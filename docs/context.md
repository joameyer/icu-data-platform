## Dissertation Architecture

### Central Thesis Question

ICU observational data is generated by a closed-loop patient–therapy system in which disease progression and therapeutic control are entangled.

The dissertation asks whether these components can be partially separated in a scientifically useful way, and whether inter-hospital treatment variation can be used as the empirical lever for doing so.

### Dissertation Logic

The current dissertation is structured as four chapters, with asymmetric importance:

#### Chapter 1 — Mortality Risk Structure / Heterogeneous Predictability
Question:
- Is ICU mortality equally modelable across patients, or is there a component of mortality that remains poorly aligned with short-horizon observable deterioration?

Role:
- Defines the limits of prediction under the available feature set.
- Establishes that not all adverse outcomes are equally accessible to data-driven modeling.
- Provides the entry point for thinking about which outcomes may or may not lie within the practical domain of the therapeutic control loop.

Important framing constraints:
- Unobserved does **not** imply unobservable.
- Any claim is bounded by the observed feature set.
- The binary subgroup framing is an expositional simplification, not biological ground truth.
- A continuum or soft assignment is acceptable and preferred over overclaiming hard classes.
- Apparent “suddenness” may partly depend on temporal granularity and charting frequency.
- The practical claim is about predictability under the data as collected, not about fundamental irreducible randomness.

#### Chapter 2 — Inter-Hospital Treatment Policy Variation
Question:
- Do hospitals apply materially different treatment policies under similar patient states, and where are those differences concentrated?

Role:
- Tests whether there is meaningful controller heterogeneity to analyze.
- Provides the empirical justification for using multi-center data as leverage in later chapters.
- May become one of the strongest standalone empirical chapters in the thesis.

#### Chapter 3 — Structurally Constrained Patient–Therapy Modeling
Question:
- Can treatment effects be partially separated from disease evolution using an information-restricted architecture?

Role:
- High-risk methodological chapter.
- Uses a two-pathway model to distinguish expected state evolution from marginal treatment-associated effects.
- This is not a causal identification claim unless much stronger assumptions are met.

Important interpretation constraint:
- The disease pathway does **not** learn untreated intrinsic physiology.
- It learns expected state evolution under the training population’s aggregate treatment distribution.
- The treatment pathway captures marginal concurrent treatment-associated effects conditional on the representation and data.

#### Chapter 4 — Cross-Hospital Validation Under Policy Shift
Question:
- Does the decomposition remain useful when transferred across hospitals with different treatment policies?

Role:
- Explicitly conditional chapter.
- Only worth pursuing if Chapters 2 and 3 establish both meaningful policy variation and at least some stable, clinically coherent treatment signal.

### Strategic Asymmetry

The thesis is not operationally symmetric across chapters.

Protected core:
- Chapter 1
- Chapter 2

High-risk extension:
- Chapter 3

Conditional bonus chapter:
- Chapter 4

This means the project is run to secure a defensible thesis even if Chapters 3–4 are weaker than hoped.

## Fallback Thesis Logic

A legitimate fallback thesis exists.

If Chapter 3 fails to produce a meaningful decomposition, and/or Chapter 4 is not justified, the dissertation can still be completed as a thesis on the **preconditions and limits of modeling patient–therapy dynamics in ICU observational data**.

That fallback is not a fake backup. It is an accepted strategic path.

Fallback structure:
- Chapter 1 as limits-of-predictability / risk-structure chapter
- Chapter 2 as controller heterogeneity / policy variation chapter
- Chapter 3 reframed as decomposition attempt, negative result, or methodological boundary analysis
- Chapter 4 omitted or replaced by integrative discussion of why robust transfer was not established

## Methodological Ground Rules

1. **Keep hypotheses explicit before methods.**  
   Each chapter must start from a defensible scientific question, not from a modeling trick.

2. **Prefer simpler accepted methods when they answer the same question.**  
   Complexity must earn its place.

3. **Do not overstate novelty.**  
   A chapter can be useful and thesis-worthy without being radically new.

4. **Do not overclaim causal structure from observational decomposition.**  
   Architectural separation is not equivalent to causal identification.

5. **Preserve external validation and reproducibility wherever possible.**  
   MIMIC-IV and possibly eICU are critical for this.

6. **Protect thesis coherence over local cleverness.**  
   Anything not serving the central patient–therapy dynamics story should be cut.

7. **Avoid sunk-cost continuation.**  
   Prior work is not included just because it already exists.

## Circularity and Validity Safeguards

- Chapter 1 risk modeling must remain conceptually distinct from Chapter 3 modeling.
- Chapter 2 policy models should not smuggle outcomes into treatment-policy characterization.
- Chapter 4, if attempted, must use hospitals held out from all relevant training stages.
- Patient-level classification or weighting in Chapter 1 must not rely on future information unavailable at the time of prediction.
- Claims in Chapter 1 must be explicitly bounded by the available variables and time resolution.
